Research at the Barkan Lab

Mycobacteria are a group of bacteria, some of them pathogenic, that cause multiple diseases in humans (and animals). The most important of these is Mycobacterium tuberculosis, the cause of tuberculosis disease (Tb). Tuberculosis used to be the #1 cause of death in Europe and North America in the 19 th century, when roughly every sixth case of death was due to it. The list of famous people who died of Tb includes John Keats, Anton Chechov, Gerge Orwell, and many others. Tb disease in a central theme in western culture, and represeted in such creations as “La Boheme”, “La Traviata”, “The magic mountain”, and “Moulin Rouge!”. Even today, around 30% of earth's population is latently infected, and around 1.5 million people die each year from Tb.

B11 loops bind to regulators sequences before the start codon in the target mRNA, affecting the translation of the mRNA by the ribosome.

Other notable mycobacteria are M. Lepra, that causes the ancient disease Leprosy; M. abscessus, causing severe lung disease in people with cystic fibrosis (CF), many times leading to their demis; and M. marinum, leading to a Tb-loke disease in fish and amphibians, and sometimes on the skin of humans.

The small RNA B11, present in all mycobacteria, was shown to regulate the translation of many genes by binding to mRNA – including ESX-4, gps-cluster and others.

In our lab, we ask the questions: How do these bacteria cause disease? How do they evade the immune system? How do they resist and tolerate antibiotics, why does it take so long to treat them, and why does the treatment fail in many cases? How do they regulate they pathogenesi, their physiology, and their metabolism? And how can we better study them, to improve our understanding of them and assist in creating better treatments and vaccines?

Example Project: the Regulation of Membrane Composition and Colony Morphology in M. Abscessus:

M. abscesuss, that causes severe lung disease in people with cystic fibrosis (CF), can form two types of colonies when grown on agar plates: smooth and rough. The bacteria that form rough colonies are more pathogenic (cause much more severe disease). “Smooth” bacteria have a special component in their cell wall, called glycol-peptido-lipid (GPL), whereas “rough” bacteria - do not. We discovered that the production of GPL can be regulated in the bacteria by several mechanisms, including a small non-coding RNA called B11, and a transcription factor called GplR1. This may enable future interventions, where we interfere with this regulation and cause the bacteria to be less virulent.

MAB_1638 is a gene coding for a TetR-like transcription factor. Deletion of this genes causes rough morphology, as there is less transcription of all the GPL-producing genes.
